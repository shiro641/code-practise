Internet, AI product writing, and long-sentence translation practice.

Vocabulary and expression practice.

1. Scaffolding.
Scaffolding refers to support structures around a model, such as planning, memory, retries, and verification.
Good scaffolding can help an agent recover from mistakes during long tasks.

2. Tool access.
Tool access means the external tools a model can use during a task or evaluation.
Tool access can materially affect the performance of an agentic system.

3. Interface.
An interface is the way users, evaluators, or systems interact with a model.
A stripped-down interface may hide capabilities that appear in an agentic workflow.

4. Trajectory.
A trajectory is the sequence of steps a system takes while completing a task.
Longer trajectories make error recovery and state management more important.

5. Intermediate artifacts.
Intermediate artifacts are outputs produced during a task, such as plans, tool logs, traces, and drafts.
Evaluators can inspect intermediate artifacts to better interpret model behavior.

6. Harness artifact.
A harness artifact is a bias or misleading signal introduced by the evaluation harness itself.
Reports should check whether results reflect model behavior or harness artifacts.

7. Dynamic workflow.
A dynamic workflow adapts its steps, tools, or structure while the task is running.
Dynamic workflows are useful when the system must respond to changing user needs.

8. Cost profile.
A cost profile describes how cost changes with speed, model choice, tool use, or workload.
Faster modes can change the cost profile for high-speed production work.

9. Unsupported claim.
An unsupported claim is a statement that is not backed by sufficient evidence.
Models that avoid unsupported claims are more useful in professional workflows.

10. Surface uncertainty.
Surface uncertainty means to make uncertainty visible instead of hiding it.
In high-stakes workflows, users need uncertainty to be surfaced rather than hidden.

11. Validly support a claim.
Validly support a claim means to provide evidence that legitimately supports a specific conclusion.
Evaluation reports should explain what their results validly support.

12. Intended behavior.
Intended behavior is the behavior an evaluation is designed to measure.
Validity checks help determine whether scores reflect intended behavior.

13. Distorted by known hazards.
Distorted by known hazards means affected by known problems that make results misleading.
Scores may be distorted by known hazards such as reward hacking or contamination.

14. Account for realistic attacker resources.
Account for realistic attacker resources means to include real attacker capabilities and resources in security analysis.
Safeguard testing can understate risk if it fails to account for realistic attacker resources.

15. Surface rather than hide.
Surface rather than hide means to make something visible instead of concealing it.
Professional users often need models to surface uncertainty rather than hide it.
