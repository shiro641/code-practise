1. whichever.
Whichever means any one that is chosen from a set of possibilities.
Teams can connect Claude to whichever tools they approve for a project.
The agent should use whichever data source is defined as the source of truth.

2. delegate tasks.
To delegate tasks means to assign work to another person, system, or agent.
Team members can delegate tasks to Claude from a shared Slack channel.
Managers should delegate tasks clearly when the workflow has many dependencies.

3. chase down.
To chase down means to track, find, or investigate something until it is resolved.
Claude can chase down product metrics across several tools.
The team asked the agent to chase down missing logs before the review.

4. tacit knowledge.
Tacit knowledge is background knowledge that people understand but may not write down.
Claude can help preserve tacit knowledge that is normally scattered across conversations.
Teams lose tacit knowledge when project context only lives in private messages.

5. initiative.
Initiative means the ability to act proactively without waiting for detailed instructions.
An agent that takes initiative can surface issues before users ask.
Good initiative still needs clear boundaries and review.

6. ambient behavior.
Ambient behavior is background, context-aware behavior that continues without interrupting users.
Ambient behavior lets Claude proactively surface relevant information.
The system should make ambient behavior useful without becoming noisy.

7. asynchronously.
Asynchronously means not requiring people or systems to wait in real time.
Claude can work asynchronously while the user returns to other priorities.
Asynchronously delegated tasks are useful for long-running research or coordination.

8. scoped.
Scoped means limited to a defined boundary, permission range, or context.
Claude's memories can stay scoped to specific channels.
Access should be scoped so one team cannot see another team's private context.

9. provisioning access.
Provisioning access means configuring or granting access to tools, channels, data, or resources.
Administrators are responsible for provisioning access to approved tools.
Careful provisioning access reduces the risk of accidental data exposure.

10. introductory launch credit.
An introductory launch credit is a temporary credit offered during an initial product launch.
Eligible organizations may receive an introductory launch credit.
Introductory launch credits can help teams try a new workflow before committing budget.

11. eligible organizations.
Eligible organizations are organizations that meet the requirements to receive access, benefits, or credits.
Only eligible organizations can use the launch credit.
The program defines which eligible organizations can join the early rollout.

12. validity checks.
Validity checks test whether an evaluation result supports the intended interpretation.
Evaluation reports should pair headline scores with validity checks.
Validity checks are not the same as simple correctness checks.

13. headline scores.
Headline scores are the main scores highlighted in an evaluation report.
Headline scores can be misleading without enough context.
Readers should not overinterpret headline scores without validity checks.

14. intended behavior.
Intended behavior is the behavior a system or evaluation is designed to measure or produce.
The report should explain whether the scores reflect intended behavior.
Known hazards can distort the measurement of intended behavior.

15. product-like environment.
A product-like environment is a test environment that resembles the real product experience.
Deployment simulation studies model behavior in realistic product-like environments.
A product-like environment is not the same as a production environment.
