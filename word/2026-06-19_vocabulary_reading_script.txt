2026-06-19 Vocabulary Reading, American English

Part A. Vocabulary and Expressions.

1. measurable impact.
Example one. A deployment should be evaluated by measurable impact, not just by model quality.
Example two. The team defined metrics to show measurable impact after the pilot.

2. customer relationships.
Example one. Strong customer relationships help partners support deployment beyond the pilot stage.
Example two. Enterprise AI programs often depend on customer relationships as much as technical expertise.

3. secure integration with company data.
Example one. Secure integration with company data allows AI systems to support sensitive internal workflows.
Example two. Without secure integration with company data, the model cannot reliably answer domain-specific questions.

4. adoption support for new ways of working.
Example one. Adoption support for new ways of working helps employees use AI in real workflows.
Example two. Training and documentation are part of adoption support for new ways of working.

5. global workforce change management.
Example one. Global workforce change management helps large organizations adopt new AI-enabled processes.
Example two. Enterprise AI programs need global workforce change management when teams operate across regions.

6. recall a commercial model.
Example one. A narrow issue may not be sufficient cause to recall a commercial model.
Example two. Regulators should require clear technical evidence before asking a company to recall a commercial model.

7. sufficient cause.
Example one. A single bug is not always sufficient cause for blocking a deployment.
Example two. The report argued that a narrow jailbreak was not sufficient cause for recalling the model.

8. deployed to hundreds of millions.
Example one. A model deployed to hundreds of millions of users requires careful safety monitoring.
Example two. Recalls become more complex when a commercial model is deployed to hundreds of millions of people.

9. life-sciences examples.
Example one. Life-sciences examples can show both the benefits and risks of advanced reasoning systems.
Example two. The evaluation used life-sciences examples to study dual-use risks.

10. malicious actors.
Example one. Malicious actors may misuse capabilities that were originally designed for beneficial research.
Example two. A threat model should account for malicious actors with realistic resources.

11. perfect jailbreak resistance.
Example one. Perfect jailbreak resistance is not currently possible for deployed AI systems.
Example two. Teams should combine safeguards and monitoring instead of assuming perfect jailbreak resistance.

12. narrow failure modes.
Example one. Narrow failure modes are easier to detect and mitigate than broad, unpredictable failures.
Example two. A defense-in-depth strategy can reduce the impact of narrow failure modes.

13. monitoring and rapid shutdown.
Example one. Monitoring and rapid shutdown can limit harm when safeguards fail.
Example two. The deployment plan paired model safeguards with monitoring and rapid shutdown.

14. data modernization.
Example one. Data modernization prepares enterprise data for safe and useful AI integration.
Example two. Without data modernization, teams may struggle to connect frontier models to internal systems.

15. operating-model redesign.
Example one. Operating-model redesign changes how roles, workflows, and decisions are organized.
Example two. AI adoption often requires operating-model redesign, not just tool installation.
