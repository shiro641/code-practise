2026-06-16 Vocabulary Reading, American English

Part A. Vocabulary and Expressions.

1. reported bypass.
Example one. The reported bypass appeared to work only in a narrow set of prompts.
Example two. Security teams should verify whether a reported bypass is reproducible before changing deployment policy.

2. broadly effective.
Example one. A broadly effective attack would work across many tasks, users, and model settings.
Example two. The issue was serious, but it was not a broadly effective method for disabling safeguards.

3. disable safeguards.
Example one. The evaluation tested whether adversarial prompts could disable safeguards in high-risk domains.
Example two. A narrow exploit should not be treated as proof that users can disable safeguards reliably.

4. thirty-day retention.
Example one. Thirty-day retention of customer data can help researchers detect and investigate jailbreak attempts.
Example two. The policy introduced thirty-day retention for safety analysis while raising privacy and cost concerns.

5. customer data.
Example one. Providers must handle customer data according to clear retention and deletion policies.
Example two. Customer data may be used for safety monitoring only under strict access controls.

6. mitigate jailbreak attempts.
Example one. The team used monitoring signals to detect and mitigate jailbreak attempts before they spread.
Example two. Safety researchers need realistic traffic data to mitigate jailbreak attempts in deployed systems.

7. technically grounded.
Example one. A technically grounded decision should rely on clear evidence about how the system actually behaves.
Example two. Regulators need technically grounded analysis before requiring a model to be withdrawn.

8. statutory process.
Example one. A statutory process can define when and how regulators may block unsafe deployments.
Example two. The company argued that such decisions should follow a fair statutory process.

9. clear technical evidence.
Example one. A claim about model risk should be supported by clear technical evidence.
Example two. Without clear technical evidence, it is hard to justify recalling a deployed commercial model.

10. unsafe deployment.
Example one. An unsafe deployment can expose users to risks that were not visible in offline evaluations.
Example two. Safety reviews should identify whether a model release would count as an unsafe deployment.

11. forward-looking statements.
Example one. Forward-looking statements describe expectations about future events rather than current facts.
Example two. Investors should not treat forward-looking statements as guarantees of future performance.

12. speak only as of the date hereof.
Example one. The statement speaks only as of the date hereof and may become outdated as circumstances change.
Example two. Legal notices often say that forward-looking statements speak only as of the date hereof.

13. life-sciences examples.
Example one. The model's life-sciences examples show how advanced reasoning can support complex research tasks.
Example two. Life-sciences examples also raise questions about dual-use risks and access controls.

14. dual-use risks.
Example one. Dual-use risks arise when the same capability can support beneficial research or harmful misuse.
Example two. Evaluators should consider dual-use risks before expanding access to advanced model capabilities.

15. defense-in-depth strategy.
Example one. A defense-in-depth strategy combines safeguards, monitoring, and rapid shutdown rather than relying on a single control.
Example two. The company used a defense-in-depth strategy to reduce the impact of narrow failure modes.
