Internet, AI evaluation, and large language model vocabulary practice.

Listen first. Then repeat after each expression and sentence.

1. Trustworthy interpretation.
Trustworthy interpretation means explaining evaluation results in a way that is supported by the available evidence.
A trustworthy interpretation avoids making claims that go beyond what the evaluation can justify.

2. Overinterpret.
Overinterpret means to draw a conclusion that is stronger than the evidence supports.
Readers may overinterpret a benchmark score if the report does not explain the tested system.

3. Lower-bound estimate.
A lower-bound estimate is an estimate that shows at least what a system can do, but not necessarily its full capability.
If performance improves with more budget, the result may be only a lower-bound estimate.

4. Credible.
Credible means realistic, believable, and supported by reasonable evidence.
Evaluations should use the strongest credible elicitation setup for the tested system.

5. Plausibly.
Plausibly means reasonably possibly, based on available evidence or context.
Capability elicitation asks whether a model can plausibly produce the capability being evaluated.

6. Threat model.
A threat model describes the assumed attacker capabilities, resources, goals, and constraints.
Safeguard testing should match the relevant threat model.

7. Adversary model.
An adversary model describes the type of attacker or misuse actor considered in a safety evaluation.
The evaluation used a stronger adversary model to test expert-level misuse risks.

8. Safeguard testing.
Safeguard testing evaluates whether filters, monitors, or blocking systems can resist target attacks or behaviors.
Weak safeguard testing can understate risk by ignoring realistic attacker resources.

9. Refusal.
Refusal occurs when a model declines to answer or perform a requested task.
Refusals can distort capability evaluations if they hide whether the model could perform the task.

10. Broken problems.
Broken problems are invalid or flawed evaluation tasks, such as tasks with wrong answers, missing files, or ambiguous prompts.
Broken problems can distort evaluation results and should be identified during validity checks.

11. Overinterpret narrow evaluation results.
Overinterpret narrow evaluation results means to treat limited evidence as if it supported a much broader claim.
Careful interpretation can prevent us from overinterpreting narrow evaluation results.

12. Account for data contamination.
Account for data contamination means to include contamination as a factor when interpreting model scores.
Evaluators should account for data contamination before interpreting high scores.

13. Distort evaluation results.
Distort evaluation results means to make evaluation results misleading or unreliable.
Reward hacking and broken problems may distort evaluation results.

14. Ignore realistic attacker resources.
Ignore realistic attacker resources means to leave out the tools, time, budget, or expertise a real attacker might have.
Safeguard testing can understate risk if it ignores realistic attacker resources.

15. Detect signs of evaluation awareness.
Detect signs of evaluation awareness means to identify evidence that a model may recognize it is being evaluated.
Reasoning traces can help evaluators detect signs of evaluation awareness.
