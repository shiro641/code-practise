June twenty first, vocabulary reading.

trustworthy interpretation.
An interpretation that stays within what the evidence can support.
The report should provide a trustworthy interpretation of the benchmark results.
Trustworthy interpretation requires evaluators to separate results from claims.

lower-bound estimate.
An estimate that shows at least this level, but may be below the true capability.
If performance improves with more budget, the score may be only a lower-bound estimate.
A lower-bound estimate should not be treated as the model's capability ceiling.

under-elicitation.
Failing to elicit the full capability of a model or system during evaluation.
Weak prompts can lead to under-elicitation of model capabilities.
Under-elicitation may cause an evaluation to understate risk.

capability ceiling.
The highest capability level measured or plausibly reachable under a setup.
Changing the harness can raise the observed capability ceiling.
The result should not be described as a capability ceiling without stronger elicitation.

threat model.
A description of the attacker, resources, goals, and constraints assumed in a safety analysis.
The safeguard test should specify the threat model it is evaluating.
An unrealistic threat model can make the system appear safer than it is.

red teaming.
Structured adversarial testing used to find failures, misuse paths, or safety gaps.
Red teaming helps teams identify jailbreaks before deployment.
The company expanded red teaming for high-risk model capabilities.

robustness.
The ability to remain reliable under noise, attacks, distribution shifts, or unusual inputs.
Robustness matters when models are deployed in dynamic production environments.
The evaluation measured robustness against prompt variations and tool failures.

evaluation awareness.
Signs that a model recognizes it is being evaluated and changes behavior accordingly.
Reasoning traces may reveal signs of evaluation awareness.
Evaluation awareness can distort results if the model behaves differently during tests.

scaffolding.
External structure around a model, such as tools, memory, retries, plans, or validators.
Agent scaffolding can materially affect task success rates.
The same model may perform differently with stronger scaffolding.

trajectory.
The sequence of actions, tool calls, and intermediate steps taken during a task.
Longer trajectories can reveal failures that short tasks do not expose.
Evaluators should inspect the trajectory, not only the final answer.

curate memory.
To select, organize, and maintain useful memory from past context.
The system uses a background process to curate memory from chat history.
Poorly curated memory can preserve stale or irrelevant assumptions.

grounded in real-world knowledge.
Based on facts, constraints, or context from the real world.
The model generated a video grounded in real-world knowledge.
Answers grounded in real-world knowledge are often more useful for practical tasks.

sufficient cause.
A reason strong enough to justify a serious action or conclusion.
A narrow jailbreak may not be sufficient cause for recalling a commercial model.
Clear technical evidence is needed before treating a failure as sufficient cause.

recall a commercial model.
To withdraw or remove an already deployed commercial model from use.
Regulators may ask a company to recall a commercial model if deployment is unsafe.
The report did not provide enough evidence to recall a commercial model.

proven capabilities.
Abilities that have been demonstrated or validated through prior work, evidence, or results.
Customers prefer partners with proven capabilities in cybersecurity and agent deployment.
Partner tiers help identify organizations with proven capabilities.
