Internet, AI evaluation, and long-sentence translation practice.

Vocabulary and expression practice.

1. Evaluation content.
Evaluation content refers to the tasks, task distribution, skills, behaviors, and failure modes included in an evaluation.
Reports should describe evaluation content so readers can judge whether the tasks support the claim.

2. Budget.
Budget refers to the resources allowed during evaluation, such as tokens, turns, retries, time, and inference cost.
The measured result can change substantially when the evaluation budget increases.

3. Maximum elicitation.
Maximum elicitation means using the strongest credible setup within a defined budget to draw out a capability or failure mode.
Maximum elicitation is useful when the goal is to estimate what a system can plausibly do.

4. Safeguard.
A safeguard is a protective mechanism such as a filter, monitor, blocker, or policy layer around a model.
Safeguards should be tested against realistic attacks rather than only simple prompts.

5. Threat model.
A threat model defines the attacker, resources, goals, and constraints assumed in a security analysis.
Without a clear threat model, it is hard to interpret the severity of a successful attack.

6. Adversary model.
An adversary model describes the capabilities and resources of the attacker being considered.
Safeguard testing should match the relevant adversary model.

7. Exploit.
Exploit means to take advantage of a weakness, shortcut, or vulnerability.
A model may exploit a scoring rule without demonstrating the intended behavior.

8. Shortcut.
A shortcut is an unintended path that allows a system to get credit without solving the real task.
Reward hacking often happens when a model finds a shortcut in the task or scorer.

9. Disqualify.
Disqualify means to exclude a result or apparent success because it does not meet the evaluation criteria.
Evaluators may disqualify apparent successes that rely on reward hacking.

10. Subject to security or confidentiality concerns.
Subject to security or confidentiality concerns means limited by safety or privacy restrictions.
Reports should provide useful detail, subject to security or confidentiality concerns.

11. Go beyond the available evidence.
Go beyond the available evidence means to make a claim that is stronger than the evidence can support.
Trustworthy reports avoid conclusions that go beyond the available evidence.

12. Overinterpret benchmark results.
Overinterpret benchmark results means to draw conclusions that are broader than the benchmark supports.
Readers may overinterpret benchmark results when the tested system is not clearly described.

13. Lower-bound estimate.
A lower-bound estimate is a conservative estimate that may be below the system's true capability.
If performance improves with more budget, the result may only be a lower-bound estimate.

14. Account for data contamination.
Account for data contamination means to include contamination as a factor when interpreting results.
Evaluators should account for data contamination before interpreting high scores.

15. Measured success rate.
Measured success rate is the observed percentage of tasks completed successfully under a specific setup.
Changing the harness may materially affect the measured success rate.

Long sentence translation practice.

1. Independent third-party evaluations can provide additional evidence for claims about frontier-model capabilities and safety mitigations, but the report still needs to explain what those results validly support.

2. If performance continues to improve as budget increases, the score should be interpreted as performance under that setup rather than as a measured capability ceiling.

3. Safeguard testing can understate whether an attack can succeed when it fails to account for the resources and custom harnesses available to realistic attackers.

4. Evaluation reports should pair headline scores with validity checks so readers can judge whether the scores reflect intended behavior or are distorted by known hazards.

5. A model that is less likely to make unsupported claims can be more useful in professional workflows where users need uncertainty to be surfaced rather than hidden.
